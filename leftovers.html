Eli's Calculus Compendium

Pythagorean Identities

Write

  • \(\sin^2(x)+\cos^2(x)=1\)
  • \(1+\tan^2(x)=\sec^2(x)\)
  • \(1+\cot^2(x)=\csc^2(x)\)

Angle Sum-Difference Indentities

Write

  • \(\sin(\alpha\pm\beta)=\sin(\alpha)\cos(\beta)\pm\cos(\alpha)\sin(\beta)\)
  • \(\cos(\alpha\pm\beta)=\cos(\alpha)\cos(\beta)\mp\sin(\alpha)\sin(\beta)\)
  • \(\tan(\alpha\pm\beta)=\frac{\tan(\alpha)\pm\tan(\beta)}{1\mp\tan(\alpha)\tan(\beta)}\)

Double Angle Identities

The double angle identities are special cases of the angle sum-difference identities that are often useful when integrating expressions involving trigonometric functions. Note that via the first pythagorean identity, two additional cosine double angle indentities can be derived.

  • \(\sin(2x)=2\sin(x)\cos(x)\)
  • \(\cos(2x)=\cos^2(x)-\sin^2(x)\)
  • \(\tan(2x)=\frac{2\tan(x)}{1-\tan^2(x)}\)

Power Reduction Identities

Write

  • \(\sin^2(x)=\frac{1-\cos(2x)}{2}\)
  • \(\cos^2(x)=\frac{1+\cos(2x)}{2}\)
  • \(\tan^2(x)=\frac{1-\cos(2x)}{1+\cos(2x)}\)

Limit Definition of the Derivative

Write

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h} \]

The Power Rule

Write

\[ \frac{d}{dx} x^n = n\cdot x^{n-1} \]

From the power rule (and the chain rule), it can be shown that the derivative of any constant is \( 0 \).

\[ \frac{d}{dx} c = 0\]

The Constant Multiple Rule

The constant multiple rule states that any constants by which function is multiplied by can be pulled out of the differential operator and multiplied by the derivative after differentiation.

\[ \frac{d}{dx}(c\cdot f(x)) = c\cdot f'(x) \]

The Chain Rule

The chain rule is used to find the derivative of composed functions.

\[ \frac{d}{dx} f(g(x)) = f'(g(x))g'(x) \]

The Sum-Difference Rule

The sum-difference rule states that in order to take the derivative of the sum or difference of two functions, simply take the derivative of each independently, then add or subtract them.

\[ \frac{d}{dx} (f(x) \pm g(x)) = f'(x) \pm g'(x) \]

The Product Rule

The product rule is used to take the derivative of two functions multiplied together.

\[ \frac{d}{dx} f(x)g(x) = f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x) \]

The Quotient Rule

The quotient rule is used to take the derivative of two functions divided by each other. Note that any derivative found by the quotient rule can also be found by the product rule, just that the quotient rule simplifies the process.

\[ \frac{d}{dx} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{f'(x)g(x)-f(x)g'(x)}{g(x)^2} \]
